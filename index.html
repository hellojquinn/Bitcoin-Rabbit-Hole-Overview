Bitcoin - An Overview.

**WORK IN PROGRESS**

Bitcoin has been named many things, such as cryptocurrency, crypto-asset, digital gold, magic internet money, energy, and so on, but what it is at its basic description is - computer code.

This computer code is so uniquely powerful because it is itself - unique.

Since the 'digital revolution' came into existence, digital 'things' can always be copied, duplicated, or manipulated. Bitcoin is the first digital 'thing' that is immune to these actions, which makes it the absolute most perfect (by definition) utility for a store of value, like money or an asset.

The reason for this perfect utility is because the creator of Bitcoin programmed this computer code to run on a technology called the Blockchain.

The Blockchain can be simply described as 'blocks' of unique computer code linked together, where each one is programmed to recognise the unique code of every 'block' that is part of the 'chain' of blocks.

New blocks are frequently added to the chain, but the unique code of a new block needs to match the pattern of the all the existing blocks, and if it doesn't, the chain of blocks will reject the new block.

The Blockchain technology used to run Bitcoin will only allow a maximum of 21 million Bitcoin to be created, meaning it is capped at this amount and the computer code will not allow more to be created.

Given that there is a capped amount, this makes Bitcoin the absolute most scarce resource in the world, by definition - when demand outstrips supply, making it a perfect utility for money and asset.

What is Bitcoin?

Bitcoin was first introduced to the public in 2009 by an anonymous developer or group of developers named Satoshi Nakamoto, publishing a white paper online detailing the design of 'digital money' that is outside of the control of any single person or organisation, removing the need for third-party involvement such as banks or government.

The method in which it does this is via a peer-to-peer (person to person) network of people running the Bitcoin blockchain on their computer systems, all connecting together as individual 'nodes', synchronising the blockchain, autonomously removing the need for third-party verification.

When Bitcoin was first created, it had no value as it was just a simple piece of computer code, but over time as awareness has grown, value has been applied once understanding the capabilities of this technology.

This initial public awareness of its value was sparked in 2010 by a transaction of 10,000 Bitcoin for 2 pizzas, valued at $41 USD.

Since that time, Bitcoin has been seen by the public as its intended design - a form of money, and has gone through myriad of volatility in its price, reaching a high of $64,400 USD in November 2021, to the present time (August 2022) at $23,327 USD, per Bitcoin.

Bitcoin has been used to purchase many commodities and services since its inception, but there is a growing understanding that Bitcoin is an asset, and as such, many people are buying their Bitcoin just to hold onto it, like they would an investable asset because of its finite amount.

Given that Bitcoin runs off a peer-to-peer network, removing the need for third-parties, it can also be used as a means of currency transfer, meaning that payment applications built on top of the Bitcoin network can accept payment in dollars, convert it to Bitcoin, transfer the Bitcoin to the recipient, and then convert that transferred Bitcoin back into dollars for the recipient, removing the requirement for financial institutions to be involved in the transaction, eliminating processing fees and times.

It is not without backlash from these financial institutions because Bitcoin disrupts their very existence, and so there have been countless smear campaigns against Bitcoin, dubbing it as a scam, worthless digital money, and that could never compete with "real money", when however the opposite is true.

What is Cryptocurrency?

Bitcoin was the first "cryptocurrency", named so because of the unique cryptographic code on each block part of the chain, and that it can be used as a form of currency. Given that the technology of blockchain was not propriety to Bitcoin itself, this has lead to the creation of many thousands of alternative 'coins' or 'Alt-Coins'.

These alt-coins are not the same as Bitcoin, and many of them spout features that are newer, different or improve on Bitcoin itself, or their purpose is entirely different to that of currency.

But the major difference is how their network operates, whether they are centralised or not. Bitcoin was the first to be 'decentralised', meaning that there is no central person, entity or place that holds control over the network.

The Bitcoin network has over 100,000 nodes across the world, a juggernaut compared to any other alt-coin attempting to be decentralised, and so majority of alt-coins are simply centralised to the people who created it, meaning that they are in control of the happenings of their coin, whereas Bitcoin cannot be controlled by anyone.

Regardless, some of these alt-coins have achieved massive success and popularity, and failures to match as well, making many people very rich in dollars, or resulting in many people losing their life savings.

Due to the amount of financial hype alt-coins create, there is a popular awareness that Bitcoin is simply the 'version 1.0' of cryptocurrency, and subsequent creations a new and improved models, like in the way any kind of industry progresses with innovation, but the opposite is true with Bitcoin.

Bitcoin was created perfect, and this is no second best.

Alt-coins are traded at a near-constant frequency as the market never closes, unlike the stock-market, and so given the extreme volatility and the lack of intrinsic value of these alt-coins, many people trade them at a rate that is akin to gambling, betting on the hope that their targeted alt-coin will go up in value, but really it is just an exchange for the buyers to cash-out in dollars, and the for alt-coin creators to convert their earnings into Bitcoin.

The buyers of these alt-coins are investing for a price of the alt-coin at the conversion back into dollars, rather than seeing the alt-coin itself as a form of money.

So while the term 'cryptocurrency' has become a huge buzzword for many people, there is still an extreme lack of understanding across the greater community of people engaged on what cryptocurrency is, and what Bitcoin actually is, at least this is the case in 2022, but awareness and education resources are growing at an exponential rate.
